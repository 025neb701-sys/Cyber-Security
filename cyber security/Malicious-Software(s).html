About the malicious software

Malicious Software, commonly called malware, is any software intentionally designed to disrupt, damage, or gain unauthorised access to a computer system. The major types include: Virus — attaches itself to a legitimate program and spreads when that program is run (requires user action to propagate); Worm — self-replicates and spreads across networks without user intervention; Trojan Horse — disguises itself as legitimate software but performs malicious actions in the background; Spyware — secretly collects user information such as keystrokes, browsing habits, and login credentials; Ransomware — encrypts files and demands payment; Adware — displays unwanted advertisements and may track behaviour; Rootkit — hides deep in the operating system, granting attackers persistent, hard-to-detect access. Malware spreads through multiple vectors: malicious email attachments, infected USB drives, compromised websites, fake software downloads, and unsecured network connections. Once installed, malware can steal data, use your computer as part of a botnet (a network of infected machines used to launch attacks on others), or completely disable your system. The impact ranges from minor annoyances (adware) to catastrophic damage (ransomware wiping out years of business data). Protection requires a multi-layered approach: antivirus software, regular system updates (to patch vulnerabilities exploited by malware), firewalls, email filtering, and most importantly, user awareness — because most malware infections begin with a human clicking something they shouldn't. ★Importance Understanding malware types helps you recognise threats and choose the right defences. A virus requires different countermeasures than a worm. Knowing how each type spreads enables better hygiene — like never plugging in an unknown USB drive or downloading software from untrusted sources. Virus Example: You download a pirated game. Hidden inside is a virus that attaches to your .exe files. Every time you run those programs, the virus copies itself further and may corrupt your data. Worm Example: The WannaCry worm (2017) spread across networks exploiting a Windows vulnerability, infecting hundreds of thousands of computers in 150 countries — including the UK's NHS — without any user action beyond being connected to a vulnerable network. Trojan Horse Example: You download a "free antivirus" app. It appears to scan your system but secretly sends your passwords and bank login credentials to a remote attacker. Spyware Example: A keylogger installed on a shared computer in a cyber café records everything a student types, including their email password and personal messages.
