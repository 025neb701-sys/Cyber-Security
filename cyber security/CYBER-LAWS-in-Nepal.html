About CYBER LAWS in Nepal

IT Act 2063 BS Electronic Transactions Digital Signature Cyber Offences Privacy Act 2075 Nepal's primary cyber law is the Electronic Transactions Act, 2063 BS (2008 AD) — commonly called the IT Act 2063. This law was enacted to regulate electronic transactions, give legal recognition to digital signatures and electronic records, and criminalise cyber offences. Under this act, activities like hacking into computer systems, publishing illegal or defamatory content online, and unauthorised interception of data are punishable offences. The act also recognises electronic contracts and digital signatures as legally valid, enabling e-commerce and digital governance. The Individual Privacy Act, 2075 BS (2018 AD) further strengthens protection of personal data, establishing rights regarding collection, processing, and sharing of citizens' personal information. From a practical standpoint, Cyber Laws in Nepal define what constitutes a cyber crime, who can investigate it (the Nepal Police's Cyber Bureau), and what penalties apply. Penalties under the IT Act include fines and imprisonment depending on the severity of the offence. For individuals, these laws mean that every online action carries legal weight — sharing someone's private photo without consent, spreading false information, or hacking a website are all prosecutable under Nepali law. It is important for students and professionals to understand these laws not only to avoid legal trouble but also to know their rights when they become victims of cyber crimes. The laws continue to evolve, with ongoing discussions about updating them to address newer threats like cyberbullying, deepfakes, and cryptocurrency fraud. ★Importance Cyber laws serve as the legal backbone of digital society. They deter potential offenders, provide victims with legal recourse, and establish standards for how digital evidence is handled in courts. Without them, cybercriminals would operate with impunity. Digital Signature Example: A company signs a contract with a government body using a digital signature verified by a licensed certifying authority. Under IT Act 2063, this signature is legally as binding as a handwritten one. Cyber Crime Case: A person creates a fake social media account impersonating a public figure and posts defamatory content. Under Section 47 of IT Act 2063, this is a punishable offence — up to Rs. 100,000 fine and/or 5 years imprisonment. Privacy Act: A company that collects users' phone numbers and sells them to third parties without consent violates the Individual Privacy Act 2075 and can face legal action.
